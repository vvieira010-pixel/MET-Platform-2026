
Reading 1: The Journey of Coffee Coffee's journey began in Ethiopia, where legend says a goat herder named Kaldi discovered its effects after noticing his goats became unusually energetic upon eating berries from a certain shrub. From there, coffee cultivation and trade moved to the Arabian Peninsula, and the drink we recognize today was first developed in Yemen during the 15th century. Muslims used it to stay awake during long night prayers, Qur'an recitation, and Islamic study sessions, which helped the beverage spread rapidly despite occasional bans by religious and political authorities.


Coffeehouses, known as qahveh khaneh, first appeared in Mecca in the 15th century and later in Constantinople in the 16th century, becoming lively centers for conversation, chess, music, and political discussion. By the early 1500s coffee had reached Cairo, Damascus, and Istanbul, and the world's first true coffeehouse is often credited as opening in Constantinople around 1475. European travelers, particularly Venetian merchants, brought coffee back to Italy in the early 1600s, and the first European coffeehouse opened in Venice in 1645. By the 17th century, coffeehouses had opened across England, France, and Germany; Britain's first coffee shop opened in Oxford around 1650–1651, followed by Pasqua Rosée's coffeehouse near London's Royal Exchange in 1652.


These establishments became known as "penny universities" because, for the price of a cup of coffee, one could sit for hours engaging in intellectual conversation, reading newspapers, and debating politics with strangers, merchants, and scholars alike. In Paris, coffee was popularized after 1669 by Suleyman Aga, the Ottoman ambassador to King Louis XIV's court, and the famous Café Procope later became a meeting place for Enlightenment intellectuals. By the mid-17th century, London alone had roughly 300 coffeehouses, each attracting a distinct crowd such as merchants, shippers, artists, or scientists. Today, coffee remains one of the most traded commodities in the world, a legacy of a trade network that began with Ethiopian highlands and Yemeni ports centuries ago.

Reading 2: Urban Gardens 
Urban gardening has grown significantly in popularity over the past decade, with an estimated 800 million people worldwide now growing food in urban areas. In cities around the world, residents are transforming empty lots, rooftops, and balconies into productive green spaces, and in the United States alone the number of community gardens has grown to over 18,000, a 25 percent increase since 2010. These gardens provide fresh produce in areas where access to quality fruits and vegetables is limited—a problem often called a "food desert"—and community gardens in North America alone are estimated to produce around 500 million dollars' worth of fresh produce annually.


Beyond food production, urban gardens offer measurable environmental benefits, including reduced air pollution, improved soil and water filtration, and a noticeable cooling effect that helps counter the urban heat island phenomenon, which affects food-desert neighborhoods especially strongly during summer months. Community gardens also strengthen social bonds: a survey by the American Community Gardening Association found that 80 percent of community gardeners reported improved social interactions and a stronger sense of community as neighbors work together to maintain shared spaces. Gardening has also been linked to mental health benefits, since contact with green environments helps people recover from stress and mental fatigue.

However, urban gardeners face real challenges, including limited space, the high cost of urban land, and soil contamination from decades of industrial pollution. Researchers studying gardens near former industrial sites, such as Seattle's South Park neighborhood, have repeatedly found concerning levels of contaminants including lead, arsenic, and petroleum residues, requiring costly remediation before food can be safely grown. Despite these obstacles, the movement continues to expand rapidly, with the global urban gardening and farming market estimated at tens of billions of dollars and projected to keep growing through the end of the decade as more households recognize the value of growing food closer to home.
